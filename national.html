Grand Canyon National Park

Grand Canyon National Park, in northern Arizona, is one of the most well known natural wonders in the United States. It features the Grand Canyon, a massive gorge carved over millions of years by the Colorado River. The canyon stretches 277 miles in length, 18 miles wide, and over a mile deep, showcasing stunning layers of red, orange, and pink rock formations that show Earth's geological history. The park offers breathtaking views, hiking trails, and opportunities for rafting and wildlife observation. It's a UNESCO World Heritage Site and draws millions of visitors annually.

Sequoia National Park

Sequoia National Park, in California's southern Sierra Nevada mountains, is renowned for its giant sequoia trees, including the largest tree on Earth by volume, the General Sherman Tree. Created in 1890, the park protects vast groves of these ancient trees, some of which are thousands of years old. In addition to the towering sequoias, the park features rugged mountain landscapes, deep canyons, and scenic rivers. Visitors can explore a range of activities, from hiking through lush forests to climbing high-altitude peaks, including Mount Whitney, the tallest mountain in the contiguous U.S. The park is also home to diverse wildlife and stunning alpine scenery.

Haleakalā National Park

Haleakalā National Park, on the island of Maui in Hawaii, is known for its striking volcanic landscapes and unique ecosystems. The park encompasses the massive Haleakalā Crater, a dormant volcano that rises over 10,000 feet above sea level, offering breathtaking views at sunrise and sunset. The summit area features otherworldly landscapes of red, orange, and black volcanic rock, cinder cones, and lava fields. In contrast, the park's lush coastal region, known as the Kīpahulu District, is rich with waterfalls, tropical rainforests, and the famous Pools of 'Ohe'o. The park is home to rare plants and wildlife, some found nowhere else on Earth, making it a must-go for nature lovers, hikers, and stargazers.
